The ERP project lifecycle

Every D365 F&O implementation follows the same arc, even when it goes by different names. This is the ten-phase view used here — pick a phase from the left to read its article, or scroll the timeline below for the full flow.

Phases

  • 1. Requirement gathering
  • 2. Fit-gap analysis
  • 3. Documentation (FRD & FDDs)
  • 4. Data templates & workshops
  • 5. Conference Room Pilots (CRPs)
  • 6. UAT 1 & user training
  • 7. UAT 2 / parallel run
  • 8. Production environment
  • 9. Go-live
  • 10. Hypercare & support
Phase 1 of 10

Requirement gathering

Discovering what the business actually needs — from declared requirements down to the unspoken ones.

Add content here: how to run discovery sessions, the difference between business and functional requirements, who attends, the artifacts produced (BRD, requirement log), MoSCoW prioritization, and the traps to avoid (blue-sky requirements, copy-paste from the legacy system).

Phase 2 of 10

Fit-gap analysis

Mapping each requirement against standard D365 F&O capability — and deciding what to do with the gaps.

Add content here: the fit-gap matrix, fit / partial fit / gap classification, the conversation around each gap (configure, customize, change the process, defer, drop), and how to write a defensible recommendation for each.

Phase 3 of 10

Documentation: FRD and FDDs

Turning agreements into binding documents — Functional Requirement Documents and Functional Design Documents.

Add content here: structure of an FRD (process, scenarios, business rules, integrations, reporting), structure of an FDD (form changes, fields, posting profile, table impact, security, test cases), sign-off process, and how to keep documents alive after sign-off.

Phase 4 of 10

Data templates & client data workshops

Defining what the client must hand over — and walking them through it before they start filling it.

Add content here: master data vs setup data vs opening balances, the data template pack (vendors, customers, items, COA, dimensions, opening balances, fixed assets), data ownership matrix, the workshop format to explain each template, validation passes, cut-off strategy.

Phase 5 of 10

Conference Room Pilots (CRPs)

Showing the configured system back to the business, with their data and their flows — early and often.

Add content here: when CRP is needed (usually CRP1 = setup demo, CRP2 = end-to-end with sample data), the agenda, who runs each scenario, how to capture feedback, and how to close out the CRP with a clear list of changes before UAT.

Phase 6 of 10

UAT 1 & user / super user training

The first formal test — business users execute scripted scenarios in a near-final environment, and learn the system as they go.

Add content here: the role of super users, training material (TTT, end-user manuals, recordings), test script structure, defect triage, how to keep UAT honest (no shortcuts from the consultant team), and exit criteria for UAT 1.

Phase 7 of 10

UAT 2 / parallel run with updated setup

A second pass with the fixes from UAT 1 — often a parallel run against the legacy system to validate end-of-period numbers.

Add content here: when a parallel run is justified, how to set it up (mirror master data, run the same period in both systems), how to reconcile differences, the typical findings (rounding, mapping, posting profile setup), and the path from UAT 2 sign-off to go-live readiness.

Phase 8 of 10

Production environment

Standing up the real environment — LCS, security, data migration, and the cut-over plan.

Add content here: LCS provisioning, sandbox vs production environments, security roles in production, who has access to do what, the cut-over plan (data freeze, opening balance load, reconciliation), and the pre-go-live checklist.

Phase 9 of 10

Go-live

The switch from old to new — typically a long weekend, with a war room, a checklist, and clear rollback criteria.

Add content here: the go-live runbook (hour-by-hour), war-room cadence, opening balance reconciliation, day-1 transactions, the "no new features" rule for the cut-over weekend, and the criteria for declaring success vs rolling back.

Phase 10 of 10

Hypercare & post-go-live support

The first 4–8 weeks after go-live, when consultant presence is highest and small problems can become big ones if missed.

Add content here: hypercare structure (daily stand-up, weekly steering), ticket triage SLAs, the first month-end close as the real test, knowledge transfer to the support team, and the formal handover from project to BAU.
